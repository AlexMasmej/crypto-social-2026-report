# Breakout v2

## Alex's framing

> **Prediction markets for CT personalities up/down future Kaito Yaps ranking.**

## Parsing

Pivot from [[Breakout v1]]'s creator-coin model to a **prediction market** specifically on **Kaito Yaps rankings**:

- **Kaito Yaps** = Kaito's leaderboard ranking crypto-Twitter (CT) personalities by engagement-weighted content metrics
- Breakout v2 = binary / directional markets: "Will @xxx rank higher next week?"
- The *commodity* being predicted is **influence position** itself

This is a **far more interesting primitive than it looks at first glance**: instead of tokenizing the person (v1) or their content ([[Zora]]) or their followers ([[2100]]), Breakout v2 tokenizes their **trajectory in a public ranking**. The ranking is maintained by a third party (Kaito), so the oracle is already solved.

## Why this is cleverer than creator-coin approaches

- **Oracle is pre-solved** — Kaito publishes the ranking; the protocol doesn't need to re-derive "who is popular"
- **Mean-reverting / momentum-native** — users can bet on continuation or correction, which is more interesting than pure appreciation bets
- **Identity-aware but not identity-tokenized** — skirts the Twitter ToS / securities issues of directly tokenizing people
- **Natural resolution cadence** — Kaito updates rankings on a schedule; markets have clean expiries
- **Speaks to a CT-native audience** — anyone who already follows Kaito Yaps is the target user

## Positioning in the space

- **Prediction-market pivot** mirrors [[Context (the team/company)]]'s arc (Context → meme.market → context.markets) and [[Foundation Labs]]' arc (Rodeo → Aura prediction markets)
- But with a **narrower, CT-native commodity** (Kaito ranks) rather than general events or pop culture
- Adjacent to [[Polymarket]] but topically focused on influencer dynamics
- Sibling-in-mechanism to [[Giggles]] (prediction market on video virality) — both are "prediction markets on attention"

## Why this matters for the wiki

**"Prediction markets on attention"** is an under-theorized primitive and deserves its own concepts/ article. Breakout v2 + Giggles + Frenzy all play in this space but with different commodity choices:
- Breakout v2: ranking position in curated leaderboard
- Giggles: virality of a specific video
- Frenzy: emergence/decay of a trend

## Post-mortem (Alex-direct, 2026-04-23)

- **Launch / status:** Launched, shut down **after 7 days** at **~$35K+ volume** (~$30K in the first two weeks).
- **Chain:** [[Base]].
- **Market structure:** **LMSR**.
- **Kaito response:** essentially nothing — volume was **too small for Kaito to care**. Oracle relationship was technically usable but there was no meaningful counterparty engagement.
- **Why shut down this early:** shipping a new product on their AI-native stack is a weeks-long (not months-long) operation, so the cost of iteration is low and "Showtime (the company) is in the business of hits, not sunk-cost coping." Next product was already in-flight.

### Product learnings

**1. Prediction markets are moments, not trends.**
The winners in this category concentrate attention into a sharp, live spike: elections, finals, breaking events. Urgency drives volume. The _worst_ markets are continuous-trend markets ("BTC in 15 min", weather) — these get arbitraged by bots and structurally lack the urgency spike. CT mindshare markets are closer to trends than moments, so were structurally capped on excitement and flow from the start.

**2. Long-tail prediction markets are a bad business.**
Even at an optimistic $1M/week volume with a 2% fee, you clear ~$1M/year in revenue. You have to own **major** markets (Polymarket / Kalshi shape) rather than try to aggregate the long tail. Attention markets are inherently long-tail — topics are many and small, with no single dominant event — so the economics are structurally bad.

There are deeper technical learnings too, but these two product truths explain most of the outcome.
